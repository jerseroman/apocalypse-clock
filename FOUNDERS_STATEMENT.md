# 🔴 Founder’s Statement

## Why I Created Apocalypse Clock

I created **Apocalypse Clock** because I believe that symbolic warnings, even powerful ones such as the **Doomsday Clock**, are no longer sufficient on their own for the complexity of the risks humanity is facing.

The deepest limitation of the Doomsday Clock is not that it is wrong. Its symbolic power is real, and its institutional authority is substantial. For decades, it has served as one of the clearest public symbols of global danger. But a symbolic clock can become too compressed for the complexity it represents.

If people only hear that the world is “closer to midnight,” the message may create anxiety, fatalism, or a short burst of media attention. It can warn, but it does not necessarily explain. It tells us that danger is high, but not enough about which threats are intensifying, which systems are failing, which risks are connected, and which human choices are making the situation worse.

That is the reason I built Apocalypse Clock.

Its purpose is not to replace the Doomsday Clock, nor to imitate it. The purpose is to add a more transparent analytical layer beneath the warning. I wanted to create a model that does not reduce global danger to a single symbolic image, but opens it into a structured map of interacting pressures: climate destabilization, geopolitical escalation, artificial intelligence risks, ecological degradation, resource stress, governance failure, technological fragility, and other systemic threats.

For me, the central issue is not only whether humanity is close to a dangerous threshold. The deeper question is whether people understand how we are approaching that threshold, and what role human behavior plays in that process.

Many of the most serious global threats are not external accidents. They are directly or indirectly shaped by human action: political decisions, institutional failures, consumption patterns, technological choices, military escalation, economic priorities, and ways of life that place increasing pressure on planetary and social systems.

Apocalypse Clock was created to make those connections more visible.

The goal is not merely to tell people that something is wrong. The goal is to help people see where the pressure comes from, how different threats reinforce one another, and why awareness without discipline is insufficient. A society cannot meaningfully reduce risks it does not understand. And it cannot mature if it treats global danger only as a spectacle, a headline, or a symbol.

Only when people understand the structure of risk can they begin to recognize their own position within it: as citizens, voters, consumers, creators, leaders, institutions, and participants in a shared planetary system.

In that sense, Apocalypse Clock is not only a warning device. It is an attempt to turn warning into understanding, and understanding into responsibility. Its purpose is to support a more disciplined awareness of human-made threats and to encourage a transition toward a more mature civilizational phase.

## Relationship to the Doomsday Clock

Apocalypse Clock is independent and is not affiliated with the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists or the Doomsday Clock.

The Doomsday Clock remains one of the most powerful symbolic warnings about global danger. Apocalypse Clock takes a different approach: it attempts to make the structure of systemic risk more visible through documented threats, uncertainty ranges, source references, scenario assumptions, and model logic.

The two projects should therefore not be understood as identical. The Doomsday Clock compresses global danger into a symbolic public warning. Apocalypse Clock attempts to unfold global danger into a more inspectable map of interacting systemic pressures.
